PC Building Guide

Understanding components, avoiding bottlenecks, and buying smart.

1. The Central Processing Unit (CPU)

The CPU is the general-purpose brain. It handles game logic, physics, OS tasks, and tells the GPU what to draw.

What to look for:

  • Core Count (Cores/Threads): Crucial for multitasking and workstation tasks (video editing, 3D rendering). For gaming, 6 to 8 cores is currently the sweet spot.
  • Clock Speed (GHz): How fast a single core operates. High clock speeds are vital for gaming, as games rely heavily on quick single-threaded calculations.
  • L3 Cache (3D V-Cache): The CPU's hyper-fast internal memory. Massive caches (like AMD's X3D chips) drastically improve gaming performance by reducing trips to system RAM.

Popular Brands:

AMD (Ryzen) vs Intel (Core). Both are excellent; check current generation benchmarks for the best price-to-performance ratio.

2. The Graphics Processing Unit (GPU)

The GPU is a specialized parallel processor containing thousands of tiny cores designed strictly to calculate pixel colors and 3D geometry incredibly fast.

What to look for:

  • VRAM (Video RAM): Textures, models, and framebuffers live here. At 1080p, 8GB is standard. At 1440p or 4K, aim for 12GB to 16GB+ or games will stutter massively.
  • Rasterization vs Ray Tracing: Rasterization is traditional rendering. Ray Tracing simulates real light physics. NVIDIA generally holds the crown for Ray Tracing performance.

The Golden Rule of Bottlenecks:

Do not pair a $1,000 GPU with a $100 CPU. The GPU will render frames so fast that it has to stop and wait for the CPU to calculate the game physics, resulting in stuttering and wasted money.

3. Memory (RAM) & Storage (SSD/HDD)

If the CPU is a chef, the SSD is the pantry (storage), and the RAM is the cutting board (active workspace).

RAM Considerations:

  • Capacity: 16GB is the minimum for modern gaming. 32GB provides a comfortable buffer for heavy games or multitasking.
  • Speed & Latency: Look for sweet spots in DDR5 (e.g., 6000MHz CL30). Extremely fast RAM yields diminishing returns.

Storage Considerations:

  • NVMe SSD: Essential. Connects directly to the motherboard via PCIe for speeds up to 7,000 MB/s. Load times become near-instant.
  • SATA SSD: Older, maxes out around 500 MB/s. Good for bulk storage.
  • HDD (Hard Drive): Spinning mechanical disks. Very slow (~150 MB/s). Only use for mass archiving of videos/photos, never install a modern game or OS on an HDD.

System Controls Manual

  • Drag & Drop: Drag components from the top-left + symbol onto the black Motherboard grid.
  • Shift + Click: Hold Shift and click two components in a row to draw a wire between them.
  • Right-Click: Delete a component or wire from the Motherboard.
  • Scroll Wheel: Zoom in and out. Zooming in reveals micro-architecture. Zooming out reveals the PCB.
  • Double-Click: Open the detailed Blueprint Modal for a component.
  • Pan Camera: Click and hold empty space to drag the camera view around.
